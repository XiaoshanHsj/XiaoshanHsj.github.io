Comparing Humans and Automatic Speech Recognition Systems in Recognition Dysarthric Speech

Data Description

The speaker-independent acoustic model are built using a subset of the TORGO database consisting of over 8400 utterances.

The SI models are trained and evaluated using the leave-one-out method. The held-out data from the test speakers is divided into an evaluation-set and an adaptation-set.

eight words: 4 phonetically similar words, 3 foils generated from the N-best list obtained from an HMM-based SR trained on the entire data.

Acoustic Features

Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs)

Linear Predictive Coding-Cepstral Coefficients (LPCCs)

Perceptual Linear Prediction (PLP) coefficients with various feature parameters, including the use of Cepstral Mean Subtraction (CMS), the use of the 0th order cepstral coefficient as the energy term instead of the log of the signal energy.

The recognition performance of the baseline of SI monophone models based on MFCC, PLP coefficients with the 0th order cepstral coefficient and LPCC are 39.94%, 39.5% and 34.33%, respectively.

comparison on speakers-adapted systems showed that PLP-based models outperformed MFCC-based systems by 2.5% absolute.

39 dimensions; 15ms for dysarthric speech; 10ms for non-dysarthric speech.

SI Baseline Models

40 left-to-right, 3-state monophone HMM and one single-state short pause model.

16 Gaussian mixture components per state.

eight-word finite-state grammar (为了对应之前的人类测试，从8个选项中选择一个正确的).

CMU pronunciation dictionary.

the baseline SI monophone models using PLP acoustic features on single-word recognition where eight alternatives are provided for each utterance is 39.5%.

Acoustic and Lexical Model Adaptation

analyze the pronunciation deviations of each dysarthric subject from the canonical form and build an associated speaker-specific pronunciation lexicon that incorporates their particular behavior of pronunciation.

Using the adaptation data from a particular speaker, we perform a two-pass MLLR adaptation: First, a global adaptation; Second, a regression class tree. This process resulted in 25.81% absolute (43.07% relative) improvement.

Using SD pronunciation lexicons, during recognition improved the word recognition rate further by an average of 3.18% absolute (8.64% relative)

In total, improved by 28.99% absolute (47.94% relative) yielding a mean word recognition accuracy of 68.39%; for non-dysarthric speech, the mean word recognition accuracy of the SI models is 71.13%. After acoustic model adaptation, the mean word recognition accuracy rise to 88.55%.
